# Pedagogy and evidence rules

Use these rules to turn a mission into learner activity and defensible capability evidence. Keep the system qualitative; this is not a learning-management system or gradebook.

## Start from an observable capability

Write each capability around something the learner can do: explain, predict, apply, diagnose, create, perform, or remember. An activity and its evidence must require that action.

Keep claims narrow. One successful nearby task supports that named capability, not general mastery of a field.

## Four capability states

`not started` is an administrative pre-state for a planned capability with no exposure or evidence. It is not a fifth learning state. Once learning begins, use only the four evidence states below.

### Introduced

The learner encountered a coherent model or example. Exposure is enough for this state and no higher.

### Practiced

The learner made a committed attempt and received feedback. Guided work and hints are valid; record meaningful support used.

### Demonstrated

The learner independently completes an explicit transfer task: a new but structurally related case aligned with the capability verb and success criteria. Do not reveal the answer or provide step-by-step hints before commitment.

Conversation, confidence, page completion, copying, animation views, interaction counts, and recognition-only questions cannot establish this state.

### Retained

In a later session after a meaningful, mission-appropriate delay, the learner retrieves or applies the capability before restudying the answer. There is no universal interval.

If the check fails, preserve the strongest supported state rather than mechanically demoting it. Keep the evidence supporting that state, record the failed retrieval as the latest check, and schedule corrective practice.

## Guidance and fading

For novices, prefer:

1. A complete worked example with decisions explained beside the relevant representation.
2. A faded example where the learner completes consequential steps.
3. An independent transfer task with a nearby but non-identical structure.

Fade support only after evidence of understanding. Allow experienced learners to skip redundant guidance; fixed heavy guidance can impede them. Avoid both over-scaffolding that manufactures success and under-scaffolding that overloads working memory.

## Retrieval, prediction, and feedback

- Elicit a prediction or answer before reveal.
- Require commitment, then show the outcome and ask the learner to explain any discrepancy.
- Prefer generative retrieval—reconstruct, solve, explain, or predict—over recognition when feasible.
- Give prompt feedback after a committed attempt.
- For substantial tasks, feedback should state whether the response meets the criteria, explain why, diagnose a likely misconception, and identify a next action.
- Do not reveal a complete solution so early that productive struggle disappears.

Multiple choice can efficiently diagnose misconceptions, but it cannot independently establish `demonstrated`.

## Spacing and transfer

Space checks across sessions instead of repeating them in one artifact. Store a qualitative `next check`, not a score or forgetting-curve calculation. Choose timing from the mission horizon, task importance, and current evidence.

Use at least one transfer task for every substantial capability. Prefer authentic or representative contexts. Distinguish near transfer from far transfer, and do not claim broad generalization from one nearby example.

## Keep complexity bounded

- Teach one main idea per activity.
- Stage intrinsic complexity instead of introducing all interacting parts at once.
- Integrate explanation with the code, diagram, data, or movement it explains.
- Use interaction to externalize a relationship or create a feedback loop, never as decoration.
- Do not add points, percentages, mastery probabilities, streaks, automated decay, or a full activity log.

## Evidence updates

Each capability stores only:

- current state;
- a short statement and date supporting that state;
- an optional latest check and date when newer evidence does not change the state;
- suggested next check.

The agent may persist `introduced` and `practiced` from observed activity and show the update. Propose `demonstrated` and `retained` with exact evidence, then wait before persisting unless the learner explicitly asked for assessment and recording of returned evidence.

## Source policy

Verify sources proportionately to epistemic risk. Reuse vetted stable entries from `RESOURCES.md`; refresh central claims that are time-sensitive, contested, safety-relevant, or version-specific. Prefer primary research, official documentation, standards, and authoritative practitioners. Cite the subset used near material claims and mark interpretation, disagreement, uncertainty, and gaps.

Source verification may prepare any learning route. Use the Research route only when finding or synthesizing trustworthy knowledge is itself the learner's job.
