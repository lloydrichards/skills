# Evidence and Sources

Make the report's epistemic posture visible. Presentation must never imply more certainty than the evidence warrants.

## Evidence states

Use a small, consistent vocabulary:

- **Verified** — directly supported by inspected source, successful test, measurement, or authoritative external source.
- **Inferred** — a reasoned interpretation from identified evidence, not directly demonstrated.
- **Open** — unanswered, untested, or awaiting confirmation.
- **Risk** — a credible adverse consequence or uncertainty that may affect the recommendation.

Explain why a state applies. A colored label is not evidence.

## Claim discipline

For every material claim:

1. State the claim precisely.
2. Identify the supporting evidence or mark it as interpretation.
3. Describe relevant limitations or counterevidence.
4. Keep the conclusion within that boundary.

Distinguish observation from evaluation. “The module has seven public methods” is an observation; “the interface is too broad” is an evaluation that needs criteria and reasoning.

## Source placement

- Put citations and source actions beside the claim they support.
- Prefer primary and authoritative sources.
- For repositories, prefer immutable revisions and precise line ranges.
- Use descriptive link text such as “Inspect cache-key construction · L12–16,” not “source” or a bare URL.
- A source index is useful for retrieval but does not replace claim-level attribution.
- Name the method and boundary when conclusions come from a sample, scan, benchmark, or test run.

## Report boundaries

State material omissions: files not inspected, tests not run, environments unavailable, data ranges excluded, or sources that could not be verified. Keep limitations visible when they change how the conclusion should be read.

## Recommendations

Connect each recommendation to:

- the finding or need that motivates it;
- the expected benefit;
- meaningful cost, trade-off, or risk;
- the owner or next action when known.

For decision reports, preserve rejected alternatives and consequences. For audits, ensure conclusions answer the stated criteria. For research reports, separate consensus, disagreement, and evidence gaps.
